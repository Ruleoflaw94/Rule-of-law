On 7 August, 2025, the Supreme Court dismissed the writ petition filed by Allahabad High Court Judge, Justice Yashwant Varma, which challenged the in-house inquiry report that indicted him in the "case-at-home" scandal. The petition also questioned the recommendation made by the then Chief Justice of India Sanjiv Khanna to the President and Prime Minister for Justice Varma‚Äôs removal.

The Court declared the writ petition non-maintainable, emphasizing that Justice Varma had voluntarily participated in the in-house proceedings before later questioning their validity. This conduct, according to the bench, amounted to acquiescence.

Nevertheless, due to the constitutional issues involved, such as judicial accountability and procedural fairness, the bench addressed five substantive issues to offer guidance for future reference. It rejected the argument that Justice Varma had a right to be heard before the report was sent to the President and Prime Minister, noting that no such procedural requirement existed.

The bench clarified that while Justice Varma had no right to a hearing at this stage, he could still defend himself if impeachment proceedings are initiated.

Facts of the Case

On March 14, during a fire-fighting operation at the official residence of Justice Varma in Delhi, a large amount of cash was discovered in an outhouse. This triggered public outrage. Subsequently, the then CJI Sanjiv Khanna formed an in-house inquiry committee consisting of Justice Sheel Nagu, Justice G.S. Sandhawalia, and Justice Anu Sivaraman.

Justice Varma was repatriated to the Allahabad High Court and his judicial duties were withdrawn. His flat denial and claims of conspiracy were deemed insufficient.

After refusing to resign when asked by the CJI, the report was sent to the President and Prime Minister. Impeachment notices have since been circulated in Parliament with the necessary support.
